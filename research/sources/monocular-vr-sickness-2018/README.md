# Monocular vs. binocular viewing and VR sickness (2018)

**Citation:** "Effects of Monocular and Binocular Viewing on Virtual Reality
Sickness while using a 3D Stereoscopic Roller Coaster Simulation."
*Investigative Ophthalmology & Visual Science (IOVS)* 59(9), ARVO Annual
Meeting abstract, 2018.
**Access:** Abstract, open
(<https://iovs.arvojournals.org/article.aspx?articleid=2694067>).
**Verification:** Verified — the study exists and the reproduced figures match
the abstract. **Caveat:** this is an **ARVO conference abstract**, not a full
peer-reviewed journal article; n is modest and self-reported.

## Scope

60 students at the Kentucky College of Optometry (34 F, 26 M; mean age
24.9 ± 4.2) rode a 3D stereoscopic roller-coaster VR simulation under two
conditions: **monocular** (eye patch over the non-dominant eye) and
**binocular** (PD aligned to the screen centers). They rated dizziness,
headaches, nausea, and eye discomfort (0–3) before and after, and reported
comfortable viewing time.

## Key points

- Subjective sickness ratings were **lower after monocular viewing** —
  dizziness 0.8 vs 1.1, eye discomfort 0.97 vs 1.2 (both statistically
  significant); headaches 0.5 vs 0.6 and nausea 0.97 vs 1.0 (not significant).
- Comfortable viewing time was slightly longer monocularly (5.1 ± 3.3 vs
  4.6 ± 3.1 min), not significant.
- Interpretation: much VR sickness is driven by 2D optical-flow/vection cues
  rather than stereoscopic disparity, so removing the second eye's input does
  not worsen — and may modestly reduce — symptoms.

## Learnings for this project

- Empirical support that **monocular users are not at a sickness disadvantage**
  in VR, and plausibly bypass the vergence–accommodation conflict entirely
  (their single eye accommodates to a fixed focal plane with no conflicting
  vergence demand). Reinforces that stereo-3D is not required for safe,
  comfortable immersive use.
- **Caveat:** subjects were binocularly-normal people *patched* for the
  monocular condition, not long-term monocular adapters; a conference abstract,
  not a full paper.
