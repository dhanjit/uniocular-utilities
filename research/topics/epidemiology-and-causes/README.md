# Topic: Epidemiology and causes

Who becomes uniocular, and why. Collated learnings; see linked source notes
for detail.

## Summary

Uniocular vision arises from trauma, enucleation, unilateral disease
(macular degeneration, glaucoma, retinal disease, diabetic retinopathy),
and developmental causes (amblyopia, congenital). Trauma is a leading cause
in working-age adults; disease dominates in older adults; amblyopia is the
most common cause in children.

## Key findings

- Roughly **30–40% of monocular blindness** is attributed to ocular trauma,
  the leading cause of monocular best-corrected blindness in working-age
  people, skewed male. (Ocular Trauma overview:
  <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4139697/>; EyeWiki Monocular
  Precautions: <https://eyewiki.org/Monocular_Precautions>)
- Disease causes (worse than 20/40 in the better eye): macular degeneration
  ~33%, glaucoma ~18%, retinal disease ~15%, diabetic retinopathy ~9%.
- Amblyopia affects up to ~4% of children — see
  [amblyopia-whole-child-2023](../../sources/amblyopia-whole-child-2023/).
- Global blindness context (~43M blind in 2020, GBD): same disease/injury
  causes drive unilateral loss. (GBD analysis:
  <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7820390/>)
