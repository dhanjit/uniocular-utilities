# Topic: Adaptation to monocular vision

How people adjust after losing vision in one eye, and how rehabilitation
helps.

## Summary

Adaptation takes months and is faster after gradual loss than sudden loss.
Formal rehabilitation is rarely provided, despite structured protocols
existing. Balance and visual-skills training help compensate.

## Key findings

- Sudden loss adapts in ~8.8 months vs. ~3.6 months for gradual loss; ~91%
  get no formal rehab — see [kraut-2002-adaptation](../../sources/kraut-2002-adaptation/).
- A multidisciplinary rehabilitation protocol (AMVR) exists and helps — see
  [ihrig-amvr](../../sources/ihrig-amvr/). Low-vision/blind programs are
  **inadequate** for uniocular patients with good central acuity.
- Balance relies on vestibular + somatosensory + visual systems; losing
  visual input forces compensation, and visual-skills training (fixation,
  scanning, pursuit, saccades) helps. (Physical Therapy and Monocular Vision
  Loss:
  <https://tlc-pt.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/6/2024/06/Physical-Therapy-and-Monocular-Vision-Loss.pdf>;
  Lighthouse Guild:
  <https://lighthouseguild.org/monocular-vision-etiology-symptoms-and-rehabilitation-2/>)

## Implications for the project

- Anchors the "first 90 days" manual: realistic timeline, what to expect,
  and active compensation strategies rather than passive waiting.
- The rehabilitation gap is the project's central justification.

## Open questions

- No validated, quantitative protocol for *training* monocular depth cues in
  newly monocular adults (see [depth-perception](../depth-perception/)).
