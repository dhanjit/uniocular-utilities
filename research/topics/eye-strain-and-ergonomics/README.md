# Topic: Eye strain, fatigue, and screen ergonomics

The single working eye does all the visual work; managing fatigue and screen
setup matters.

## Summary

Asthenopia is highly prevalent and tied to sustained near work; the 20-20-20
rule and good monitor ergonomics mitigate it. Uniocular-specific strain
(uncoupled accommodation, maladaptive posture) is plausible but
under-studied.

## Key findings

- Pooled asthenopia prevalence: eye tiredness ~65%, strain ~47%; unequal
  refractive error contributes — see [asthenopia-meta-analysis-2026](../../sources/asthenopia-meta-analysis-2026/).
- The **20-20-20 rule** and structured breaks reduce strain. (EyeWiki:
  <https://eyewiki.org/Asthenopia>)
- Monitor ergonomics: ~arm's length, top of screen at/just below eye level,
  primary monitor centered; lower brightness, larger fonts, higher contrast.
  (OHCOW: <https://www.ohcow.on.ca/office-ergonomics-reference-guide/computer-screens-monitors/>;
  Ergonomics Plus: <https://ergo-plus.com/office-ergonomics-position-computer-monitor/>)
- **Uniocular-specific hypothesis** (provisional, from the
  [synthesis](../../deep-research-synthesis.md)): with vergence irrelevant,
  the single ciliary muscle bears uncoupled sustained accommodation; field
  loss may drive lean-in / head-tilt postures and secondary neck strain.

## Implications for the project

- Evidence base for the **eye-strain-manager** (20-20-20 timer, contrast and
  text presets) and the screens/computing manual.

## Open questions

- No biometric data isolating ciliary-fatigue rate in the single working eye
  vs. binocular peers — a flagged gap.
