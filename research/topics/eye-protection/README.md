# Topic: Protecting the remaining eye

The single most consequential safety topic: the remaining eye is
irreplaceable, so injury prevention is paramount.

## Summary

Standard of care is polycarbonate protective eyewear worn at all times, with
rated protection for sport. Compliance is a behavioral problem; repeated
clinical counseling is the main proven lever.

## Key findings

- **Polycarbonate lenses, worn at all times** (even at home); prescriptions
  should specify polycarbonate; keep a spare. (EyeWiki:
  <https://eyewiki.org/Monocular_Precautions>; AAO:
  <https://www.aao.org/eye-health/symptoms/injuries-protective-eyewear>)
- **Sport/high-risk activities require rated protection**; functionally
  one-eyed people (best-corrected acuity worse than 20/40 in the poorer eye)
  should avoid boxing/full-contact martial arts. (AAO clinical statement:
  <https://www.aao.org/education/clinical-statement/protective-eyewear-young-athletes>)
- **Compliance barriers are psychosocial**, and counseling frequency
  predicts compliance — see [neimkin-custer-2017-eyewear-compliance](../../sources/neimkin-custer-2017-eyewear-compliance/).
- **Pediatric compliance** is higher but imperfect (~35% wore protection ≥90%
  of the day in one study). (PMC:
  <https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12238169/>; CHECT:
  <https://chect.org.uk/protective-eyewear-for-children-with-monocular-vision/>)

## Implications for the project

- Likely the **highest-impact manual**.
- Any reminder/habit tooling must address aesthetic objections and rely on
  repeated reinforcement, not one-time information.

## Open questions

- Which behavior-change interventions best sustain adult compliance.
