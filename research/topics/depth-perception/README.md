# Topic: Depth perception and monocular cues

Uniocular people lose stereopsis but retain monocular depth cues — motion
parallax (driven by head movement) is the most powerful.

## Summary

Depth can be partially recovered through monocular cues, especially motion
parallax from translational head movement. This is trainable in principle,
though formal adult training protocols are lacking. Unequal eyes can produce
a Pulfrich motion-in-depth hazard.

## Key findings

- Motion parallax needs translational movement; the smooth-pursuit signal
  resolves depth-sign — see [motion-parallax-pursuit-2014](../../sources/motion-parallax-pursuit-2014/)
  and the stability-zone thresholds in [ono-ujike-2005-motion-parallax](../../sources/ono-ujike-2005-motion-parallax/).
- One-eyed people regain functional depth by keeping the head in motion
  (often unconsciously), but parallax does not fully replace stereopsis.
- Parallax can **augment** other depth cues (shown in macular degeneration:
  <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12849822/>).
- **Pulfrich effect:** unequal interocular brightness/contrast creates false
  motion-in-depth, with little neural adaptation — see [pulfrich-monovision-2013](../../sources/pulfrich-monovision-2013/).
- The monocular cue set (relative size, occlusion, shadow/shading, texture
  gradient, aerial/linear perspective) is teachable. (Demos:
  <https://isle.hanover.edu/isle2/Ch07DepthSize/Ch07MonocDepthInv_evt.html>)

## Implications for the project

- Core evidence for the **depth-cue trainer** (parallax exercises tuned to
  head-movement speed) and the **depth-cue rendering** module.
- Pulfrich is a specific caveat for people with partial second-eye vision.

## Open questions

- Effectiveness of deliberate depth-cue training in newly monocular adults
  is largely unproven.
