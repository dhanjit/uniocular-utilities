# Topic: Driving with monocular vision

## Summary

Evidence is mixed and hinges on adaptation: sudden monocularity sharply
degrades driving, while established, adapted monocular drivers can perform
comparably. The Pulfrich effect is a specific hazard for people with unequal
eyes. Licensing rules vary by region.

## Key findings

- Sudden monocular condition: **2.1–6.5×** collision risk, reaction time
  **+64–126 ms**; a sudden monocular *reduction* with field preserved did
  **not** impair performance — see [racing-drivers-2019](../../sources/racing-drivers-2019/).
- Population studies of commercial drivers conflict (more crashes vs.
  equivalent rates). Many jurisdictions screen with both eyes or the better
  eye. (Vision and Driving: <https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2975746/>)
- Blind-side scanning predicts hazard detection — see [driving-hemianopia-vii-2021](../../sources/driving-hemianopia-vii-2021/).
- Pulfrich motion-in-depth hazard for unequal eyes — see [pulfrich-monovision-2013](../../sources/pulfrich-monovision-2013/).

## Implications for the project

- Driving guide: emphasize the adaptation period, mirror setup, deliberate
  blind-side scanning, the Pulfrich caveat, and "check local law."
- Avoid blanket fitness-to-drive or legal advice.

## Open questions

- How quickly newly monocular drivers reach "adapted" performance, and how to
  measure readiness.
