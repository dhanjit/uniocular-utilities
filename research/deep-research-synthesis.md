## Scope distinction (carried from the report)

Functionally uniocular individuals are distinct from low-vision or totally
blind populations: they typically retain normal or optimally-corrected
acuity in the working eye. Low-vision interventions (magnification,
contrast filters, narration) are largely misaligned for them. Their four
specific challenges are: (1) absolute loss of stereopsis; (2) a
geometrically reduced horizontal field with a persistent blind side and
loss of the temporal crescent; (3) fatigue of the single working eye from
uncoupled sustained accommodation; and (4) protecting the single
irreplaceable remaining eye.

## Part A — Synthesis of core literature

**1. Adaptation to monocular vision** — Kraut & Lopez-Fernandez,
*International Ophthalmology Clinics*, 2002 (PMID 12131596). A retrospective
30-question survey of 65 patients who lost an eye (enucleation / phthisis
bulbi). Sudden loss adapted in ~8.8 months on average vs. ~3.6 months for
gradual loss — suggesting the cortex re-weights monocular cues before total
unilateral loss. Reported sequelae: depth-perception difficulty, cosmesis
concerns, reading strain, and serious psychosocial consequences
(depression, job loss, alcoholism). Notably, ~91% received no formal
rehabilitation. Limitations: retrospective, recall bias, subjective survey
rather than objective visuomotor tracking.

**2. Motion parallax driven by head movements** — Ono & Ujike, *Perception*,
2005 (PMID 15943054). Three psychophysics experiments yoking a stimulus to
lateral head movement; 3–6 observers each. Found a "stability zone" where
depth is perceived without concomitant motion (location constancy); slower
head movements need greater parallax magnitude for equivalent perceived
depth. Beyond threshold, the illusion breaks into concomitant motion.
Limitation for our purposes: tested patched binocularly-normal subjects on
CRT displays, not long-term monocular adapters.

**3. Vision and visual history in elite athletes** — Barrett et al.,
*Sports Medicine - Open*, 2017 (PMID 29127516; open access). Cross-sectional
study of 59 athletes (16 elite female cricketers, 23 near-elite male
cricketers, 20 rugby-league players); measured acuity, colour, TNO near
stereoacuity. ~27% had sub-optimal habitual on-field vision (mostly
uncorrected ametropia). Elite cricketers had better-than-norm
stereoacuity (p < 0.001), but rugby and near-elite cricketers did not
exceed norms. Takeaway for uniocular users: absent perfect stereopsis does
not preclude high-speed interception; compensation via looming and
relative size is plausible. Limitation: no dynamic visual-acuity /
motion-in-depth measures.

**4. Functional consequences of amblyopia** — Kelly et al., *Vision
Research*, 2025 (PMID 40319624). Integrated visuo-cognitive, motor, and QoL
measures in 114 adults (23 pure amblyopia, 52 amblyopia+strabismus, 20
strabismus, 19 controls). Amblyopia interferes with contrast sensitivity,
eye–hand coordination, and reading (≈122.7 wpm, below binocular norms), with
depressed psychosocial scores. Caution mapping to *acquired* uniocularity:
amblyopia involves cortical suppression / developmental deficit, whereas an
enucleated adult retains a fully developed cortex and fatigues mainly from
monocular accommodation and scanning. End-points (reading fatigue, slower
fine motor, reduced QoL) nonetheless converge. Limitation: mixed
strabismic/amblyopic cohorts.

**5. Impact of an ocular prosthesis on QoL** — Makrakis et al., *Journal of
Prosthodontics*, 2021 (DOI 10.1111/jopr.13332). Prospective longitudinal
trial (SF-36, PSS-10) in 26 anophthalmic patients at pre-install, 3, and 6
months. Significant gains in SF-36 "Bodily Pain" and "General Health"; no
significant reduction in global perceived stress; chronic localized
discharge common, without severe inflammation. Limitation: generic
instruments not validated for anophthalmia.

**6. QoL living with ocular prosthesis (editorial)** — Goiato et al.,
*Expert Review of Ophthalmology*, 2018 (DOI 10.1080/17469899.2018.1503534).
**Non-peer-reviewed narrative review.** Argues prostheses are functionally
critical (preventing eyelid deformation, orienting lacrimal flux, restoring
orbicularis tone), not merely cosmetic; un-rehabilitated anophthalmia can
drive social-avoidance ("anthrophobia"). Limitation: editorial, no primary
data.

**7. Vision-rehabilitation team management of AMV** — Ihrig, 2013
(ResearchGate 235522108; see also the open VA write-up linked in the main
review). Describes the multidisciplinary Acquired Monocular Vision
Rehabilitation (AMVR) protocol (low-vision optometry + O&M + blind-rehab +
psychosocial counseling). Presented as a single-patient case over ~1 year;
reported gains in confidence, near-space motor skill, and reduced
depression. Limitation: single anecdotal case at a late adaptation stage.

## Part B — Under-researched domains

**1. Depth-cue / motion-parallax training in adults** — *Evidence:
extremely limited.* No standardized clinical protocol exists; adaptation is
largely self-taught (consistent with Kraut's 91%-untrained finding).
Foundational feasibility exists (Ferris 1972 voluntary-head-movement depth
gains; US Navy underwater parallax-training work; Dorethy's pediatric
parallax-cue film training) — *these older/specialized citations are
unverified here* — but translation to a modern adult post-enucleation
pathway is unrealized.

**2. Long-term single-eye screen strain** — *Evidence: strong physiological
rationale, little monocular-specific longitudinal data.* For binocular
users, digital eye strain is largely a vergence–accommodation conflict; for
uniocular users vergence is irrelevant, so the single ciliary muscle bears
uncoupled sustained accommodation, plausibly fatiguing faster. Restricted
field + absent stereopsis may also drive maladaptive postures (leaning in,
head tilt/torticollis) and secondary cervical/shoulder strain. (CVS "up to
69%" general-population figure reproduced as reported; unverified.)

**3. Protective-eyewear compliance** — *Evidence: robust retrospective data
on barriers; few prospective intervention trials.* Neimkin & Custer 2017
(n=132): 79.7% compliance among prior glasses-wearers vs. 67.1%
non-compliance among prior non-wearers. Non-compliance reasons: "lack of
concern" (43%), aesthetics (26%), perceived unnecessary because acuity is
already sharp (17%). Pediatric compliance higher (a 33-child study: ~85%
wore protection ≥50% of waking hours; near-universal during sport). A
significant correlation between number of clinical encounters and
compliance points to repeated structured counseling as the main proven
lever.

**4. AI/AR assistive tech for uniocular users** — *Evidence: early
prototype phase; market biased to binocular or fully-blind users.* Real
needs are blind-side field mitigation and near-space depth mapping. Optical
state of the art is Peli's **multiplexing prism** (field expansion without
apical scotoma). AR direction: front-camera "picture-in-picture" of the
blind side rendered onto the seeing field (hemianopia trials show mobility /
hazard-detection gains). AI **monocular depth estimation** (2D→3D via neural
nets; ARKit/ARCore) exists, but consumer software translating it into
alternative feedback (haptic, colour-mapping) for users lacking stereopsis
is essentially missing — a clear build opportunity.

## Part C — Standards and certification

Existing accessibility frameworks treat vision impairment as an
acuity-reduction scale and do not codify the needs of users with perfect
acuity but zero binocular disparity.

- **W3C XAUR** (XR Accessibility User Requirements, 2021) — 19 immersive
  user needs; does not isolate stereopsis loss, but its routing of
  environmental alerts to secondary text/haptic channels could be adapted
  to blind-side alerts. <https://www.w3.org/TR/xaur/>
- **Game Accessibility Guidelines** — pragmatic engine-level items highly
  relevant though not labelled monocular: mono/stereo audio toggle (note:
  blast/impact trauma often pairs eye loss with same-side hearing loss),
  adjustable FOV sliders, radar/off-screen indicators for blind-side
  events. <https://gameaccessibilityguidelines.com/full-list/>
- **WCAG 2.2** — almost entirely 2D-DOM focused; no guidance for 3D / z-axis
  / stereoscopic dependencies.

**Existing certifications:** none. No scheme globally certifies a product as
"compatible with monocular/one-eyed users."

### Proposed Uniocular Compatibility Standard (UCS)

A concise, testable, WCAG-style self-certification scheme:

**Level A — core safety & usability**
- *1.1 Stereo-independence.* No critical task/UI/navigation relies
  exclusively on stereopsis.
- *1.2 Hardware agnosticism.* XR/VR must launch and remain operable in a 2D
  / monocular output mode; must not force stereoscopic dual-lens rendering.
- *1.3 Audio spatialization toggle.* 3D spatial audio must offer a mono mix
  or highly visible directional visual indicators for critical sounds.

**Level AA — enhanced spatial accessibility**
- *2.1 Blind-side event indicators.* Critical events/hazards outside the
  central ~60° field trigger a centralized visual/auditory/haptic
  directional cue.
- *2.2 Adjustable FOV.* 3D apps provide a user-adjustable FOV slider to
  compensate for horizontal field loss.

**Level AAA — optimal ergonomics & XR fidelity**
- *3.1 Monocular depth-cue enhancement.* Toggle for forced high-contrast
  shadows, atmospheric perspective, and exaggerated motion parallax on
  lateral head tracking.
- *3.2 Fatigue mitigation (fixed focal planes).* Sustained reading
  surfaces in XR/3D can be locked to a fixed focal distance to reduce
  continuous accommodation.

### Task-difficulty matrix (stereopsis dependency)

| Task | Impairment | Rationale |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Reaching/grasping (near field) | High | Peripersonal-space fine motor relies on disparity; slower, wider grip apertures pre-contact |
| Pouring / threading | High | Near-space alignment with head still → little parallax; leans on stereopsis |
| Walking / gait | Moderate | Step-height/curb estimation; more cautious gait, higher clearances |
| Dynamic interception (ball sports) | Moderate | Compensable via looming / motion-in-depth |
| Driving (lane change / tight spaces) | Moderate–High | Loss of ~35° temporal crescent + near-space depth for alignment |
| Distance judgment (> 6 m) | Essentially unaffected | Stereopsis negligible beyond ~6–10 m; pictorial cues suffice |
| Reading on 2D screen | Unaffected acuity / affected by fatigue | 2D screen carries no disparity; fatigue from sustained accommodation |

## Key evidence gaps (from the report)

1. **Digital accommodation-strain models** — no biometric quantification of
   ciliary fatigue rate in the single working eye vs. binocular peers.
2. **Motion-parallax VR/AR efficacy in rehab** — no trials testing whether
   exaggerated parallax in consumer headsets improves navigation or shortens
   adaptation for newly enucleated adults.
3. **Standardized clinical rehab protocols** — no validated quantitative
   monocular depth-cue training protocol post-trauma.
4. **Haptic blind-side feedback** — sensory-substitution systems tuned to the
   ~35° monocular blind side are largely absent from peer-reviewed work.
5. **Pediatric vs. adult protective-compliance psychology** — little
   qualitative work on why adult compliance drops, and which behavioral
   interventions best sustain adherence.
